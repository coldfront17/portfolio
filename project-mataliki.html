Matariki Hunga Nui: Mobile Learning Experience

Role / Type
Principal Investigator, UX Design Lead / Funded Research
Platform
Mobile (iOS / Android)
Timeline
Jan 2023 – Apr 2024
Funding
NZD $200,000, MBIE SfTI National Science Challenge

Matariki Hunga Nui is a mobile app designed to support rangatahi (Māori youth) engagement with Māori astronomical knowledge — Tātai Arorangi — through 360-degree video, audio narration, and intuitive mobile interfaces. The project was funded as part of the SfTI National Science Challenge, with NZD $200,000 in funding secured through a proposal I wrote and led as Principal Investigator. I was responsible for both the research direction and the end-to-end product design.

The design process began with co-design workshops with Māori community groups and iwi partners, which established the cultural requirements and content priorities before any interface design work started. This was non-negotiable: designing for a culturally specific audience meant every interaction model and content decision needed to be validated with the community, not assumed. From those workshops I developed the information architecture and navigation model for the app, balancing accessibility for users with limited smartphone experience against the richness of the astronomical content. I ran multiple rounds of prototype iteration, incorporating feedback from both community stakeholders and youth users throughout. One of the core design challenges was making 360-degree video work smoothly on consumer mobile hardware without sacrificing the immersive quality that made the content meaningful. I worked closely with the development team from early in the process to understand the constraints and design within them, rather than treating technical limitations as a late-stage problem.

User testing was conducted with approximately 20 participants across different age groups and digital literacy levels, with sessions designed to surface both usability issues and cultural appropriateness concerns. Findings from each round fed directly into design revisions before the next iteration. Managing a cross-functional team — including a developer, a Māori artist, and cultural advisors — while also acting as the design lead required clear communication, structured milestone planning, and a high degree of trust with all stakeholders.

The app was delivered on time and within budget, receiving strong usability and satisfaction ratings from community stakeholders and youth participants in user testing. The project established a replicable model for culturally grounded co-design in mobile product development, and contributed to a broader five-year national research programme on regenerative tourism and immersive technology.
